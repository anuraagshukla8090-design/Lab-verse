# TIG Welding Station

## Overview

The TIG (Tungsten Inert Gas) Welding Station, formally known as a Gas Tungsten Arc Welding (GTAW) station, is a precision welding workstation featuring a high-frequency arc-starting power source, a non-consumable tungsten electrode torch, a separate filler rod, an inert shielding gas supply, and a foot or hand amperage controller. TIG welding produces the highest-quality, cleanest welds of all arc welding processes and is used extensively wherever weld aesthetics, metallurgical integrity, and dimensional precision are paramount.

In an educational mechanical laboratory, the TIG welding station develops the highest level of manual dexterity and process understanding of any welding module. Students must simultaneously manage torch angle, arc length, filler rod addition, and amperage control — often with both hands and a foot pedal — while interpreting the weld pool in real time. This multi-variable coordination challenge makes TIG welding an effective capstone exercise after students have developed foundational skills on MIG or arc welding stations.

The work enabled at this station spans precision joint welding on thin-gauge stainless steel, aluminium, titanium, copper alloys, and exotic alloys such as Inconel. Applications include aerospace structures, surgical and food-grade fabrication, motorsport components, artistic metalwork, and precision pipe welding. The TIG station is also used to deposit weld passes for metallurgical analysis in materials science coursework, making it indispensable across mechanical, aerospace, and materials engineering programs.

## Working Principle

TIG welding sustains a controlled electric arc between a non-consumable tungsten electrode and the base metal workpiece. Because tungsten has the highest melting point of all metals (3,422 °C), it resists melting and maintains a stable arc geometry. The arc itself reaches temperatures of 6,000–8,000 °C, sufficient to melt virtually any engineering metal. Filler metal, when required, is manually added by the welder dipping a separate filler rod into the leading edge of the weld pool.

An inert shielding gas — typically pure Argon, or Argon/Helium mixtures for higher heat input and penetration — flows through the torch body and ceramic cup (nozzle) to blanket the electrode, arc, and weld pool from atmospheric contamination. Because no flux is used, TIG welds are exceptionally clean, with no slag inclusion risk and minimal fume generation (compared to SMAW).

For aluminium and magnesium welding, AC (alternating current) is used: the electrode-positive half-cycle performs **cathodic cleaning**, breaking up the refractory aluminium oxide (Al₂O₃) surface layer that would otherwise prevent fusion. For steel, stainless steel, and titanium, DCEN (DC Electrode Negative) is used, concentrating maximum heat in the workpiece for efficient penetration. A high-frequency (HF) arc-start circuit initiates the arc without contact, preventing tungsten contamination of the weld pool.

## Main Components

| Component | Function |
|---|---|
| TIG Welding Power Source (AC/DC) | Provides precisely controlled AC or DC welding current with HF arc-start and slope-in/slope-out controls |
| TIG Torch Body | Insulated handle housing the collet, collet body, and back cap; connects to gas, water (if water-cooled), and power |
| Tungsten Electrode | Non-consumable electrode (pure, ceriated, or thoriated tungsten) that sustains the arc |
| Collet and Collet Body | Clamping mechanism inside the torch that grips and makes electrical contact with the tungsten electrode |
| Ceramic Nozzle (Cup) | Directs and concentrates shielding gas flow around the arc and weld pool |
| Gas Lens | Mesh screen assembly that converts turbulent gas flow into a laminar, effective shielding envelope |
| Foot Pedal / Hand Control | Allows the operator to modulate welding current in real time during the weld |
| Shielding Gas Cylinder & Regulator | Supplies and regulates pure Argon or Argon/Helium at 6–12 L/min flow rate |
| Filler Rods | Separately held consumable rods matched in composition to the base metal |
| Back Purge Fitting | Delivers shielding gas to the back (root) side of pipe or tube welds to prevent oxidation |

## Inputs and Outputs

### Inputs
- Base metal workpieces (stainless steel, mild steel, aluminium, titanium, copper, exotic alloys)
- Non-consumable tungsten electrode (correctly ground/prepared for the material)
- Filler rods (ER308L for stainless, ER4043/ER5356 for aluminium, ER70S-2 for mild steel)
- Shielding gas (pure Argon or Ar/He blend) at 6–12 L/min
- Electrical power (230 V single-phase or 415 V three-phase)
- Operator inputs: amperage (via foot pedal), travel speed, filler rod feed rate, torch angle

### Outputs
- High-quality, slag-free fusion weld bead
- Minimal welding fumes (inert gas shielded, flux-free process)
- Heat-affected zone (narrower than SMAW and GMAW due to concentrated arc)
- Spent filler rod stubs
- Depleted shielding gas

## Operating Procedure

1. **Pre-use inspection**: Check torch body for cracks, inspect all gas fittings and hoses, confirm tungsten electrode is properly installed and seated in the collet.
2. **Select and prepare the tungsten electrode**: Choose the correct type and diameter for the material (e.g., 2% ceriated for DC steel/stainless; pure or zirconiated for AC aluminium). Grind the electrode to a sharp point (DC) or balled tip (AC) as required.
3. **Install the tungsten**: Insert the electrode into the collet, leaving approximately 3–5 mm stick-out from the ceramic cup. Tighten the back cap.
4. **Select the correct filler rod**: Match filler composition to base metal. Lay filler rods flat and clean with acetone before use.
5. **Set power source parameters**: Select AC or DCEN, set amperage range (typically 1 A per 0.025 mm of material thickness as a starting rule), set pre-flow (1–2 s) and post-flow (5–10 s) gas timers, and set HF arc start mode.
6. **Open shielding gas cylinder and set flow rate** to 6–10 L/min (larger cup diameters may require 10–15 L/min). Confirm gas flows from the torch when triggered.
7. **Prepare the workpiece**: clean all surfaces with acetone and a stainless steel wire brush (dedicated per alloy to avoid cross-contamination). Tack-weld or fixture the joint.
8. **Don all PPE**: welding helmet (shade 10–12), TIG gloves (lighter/more flexible than SMAW gloves to allow filler rod control), fire-resistant clothing, safety boots. Activate fume extraction.
9. **Position the torch**: hold at 75–80° from the work surface (nearly vertical), with the electrode 1.5–3 mm above the base metal.
10. **Initiate the arc**: press the foot pedal or trigger. The HF circuit will start the arc without contact. Increase amperage to operating level via the foot pedal.
11. **Establish the weld pool**: dwell briefly at the start to build a pool of the desired diameter (typically 2–3× the material thickness).
12. **Add filler rod**: with the other hand, introduce the filler rod at 15–20° to the work surface, dipping the tip into the front of the pool rhythmically. Never let the rod contact the tungsten.
13. **Advance the torch** steadily along the joint while maintaining consistent arc length, pool size, and filler addition rhythm.
14. **Terminate the arc**: ramp amperage down via the foot pedal to avoid crater cracking, then lift off. Allow post-flow gas to protect the hot weld and electrode.
15. **Inspect the weld**: examine for discolouration (oxidation indicates gas coverage failure), porosity, undercut, and incomplete fusion. Clean the tungsten tip if contaminated and re-grind before continuing.

## Safety Rules

1. **Never look at the TIG arc without a shade 10–12 lens**: TIG produces intense UV and visible radiation even at low currents; arc eye can occur from indirect or reflected exposure.
2. **Do not touch the tungsten electrode or workpiece immediately after welding**: the tungsten and adjacent base metal remain at hundreds of degrees for several minutes after arc extinction.
3. **Always use HF arc start**: contacting the tungsten to the base metal to start the arc contaminates the tungsten with base metal inclusions and introduces tungsten particles into the weld — a serious defect.
4. **Keep thoriated tungsten electrodes away from food and skin contact** and handle with clean gloves; thoriated tungsten (ThO₂) is mildly radioactive. Ceriated electrodes are a safer alternative.
5. **Do not allow the filler rod trailing end to leave the gas shield** while hot; withdraw it slowly and keep it in the post-flow gas stream to prevent oxidation and contamination.
6. **Secure all gas cylinders** upright with chains or brackets at all times.
7. **Ensure adequate ventilation and fume extraction**: although TIG fumes are lower volume, they can contain chromium VI (from stainless steel) and ozone, both hazardous.
8. **Keep the ceramic nozzle clean** and inspect before each use; a cracked nozzle disrupts gas coverage and causes immediate weld oxidation.
9. **Never weld titanium without full inert gas coverage**: titanium oxidises rapidly above 400 °C; a trailing shield and/or back purge fixture is mandatory for titanium work.
10. **Conduct a gas leak test** before welding: spray the regulator, hoses, and torch connections with soapy water and look for bubbles.
11. **Store filler rods vertically or horizontally** in their original tubes; contaminated or bent filler rods introduce porosity and inclusions.
12. **Disconnect power before changing the electrode, collet, or nozzle** to eliminate any risk of accidental arc initiation.

## Common Applications

- Precision stainless steel fabrication (food-grade, pharmaceutical, chemical plant fittings)
- Aluminium alloy welding for aerospace and motorsport structures
- Titanium and exotic alloy welding experiments in materials science coursework
- Root pass welding on pipe and tube joints (prior to MIG fill and cap passes)
- Artistic and decorative metalwork using ferrous and non-ferrous metals

- Thin-gauge sheet metal work (< 2 mm) where heat control is critical
- Dissimilar metal welding experiments with appropriate filler selection

## Maintenance

- **After each use**: Remove and inspect tungsten electrode; clean or re-grind if contaminated. Wipe torch body exterior and inspect ceramic cup for cracks.
- **Daily**: Check gas fittings for leaks with soapy water; inspect torch cable for kinks or insulation damage.
- **Weekly**: Clean collet and collet body with a dry cloth; inspect and clean the gas lens mesh for metallic debris.
- **Monthly**: Inspect power source connectors and ground lead; blow out the power source interior with dry compressed air (machine off and unplugged).
- **As needed**: Replace cracked or chipped ceramic cups; replace collets if the bore is worn and the tungsten slips; replace gas lens if mesh is deformed.
- **Filler rod management**: Store all filler rods by alloy in clearly labelled tubes with end caps to prevent contamination and moisture exposure.
- **Tungsten inventory**: Maintain a stock of correctly typed and sized tungsten electrodes; discard contaminated electrodes rather than welding with them.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Why does my tungsten keep balling up instead of maintaining a point?**
Tungsten balling occurs during DC welding when the electrode is not appropriate for DC use (e.g., pure tungsten, which is designed for AC aluminium work) or when the amperage is too high for the electrode diameter. For DC SMAW/TIG work, use 2% ceriated (green/grey band) or 2% thoriated (red band) tungsten and ensure the diameter matches the current range (e.g., 2.4 mm tungsten for 60–180 A).

**Q: Why is my weld turning blue/purple or going black on stainless steel?**
Colour change on stainless welds indicates oxidation, which means the shielding gas coverage has failed. This can be caused by insufficient gas flow, a cracked or incorrect-size ceramic cup, a gas lens missing or blocked, draughts disturbing the gas envelope, or too long an arc length. Increase flow rate to 10–12 L/min, use a larger cup with a gas lens, and shield the area from air movement.

**Q: What is the purpose of the AC balance control on aluminium welding?**
AC balance adjusts the proportion of each AC half-cycle: the electrode-positive (EP) half-cycle performs cathodic cleaning of the aluminium oxide layer; the electrode-negative (EN) half-cycle provides penetration. Increasing the EP% gives better cleaning but more heat in the electrode (requiring a larger tungsten). Increasing the EN% gives deeper penetration with less cleaning. A typical starting balance for aluminium is 30–40% EP.

**Q: How do I avoid tungsten contamination of the weld pool?**
Contamination occurs when the tungsten tip touches the filler rod or the weld pool, transferring tungsten inclusions (a serious weld defect). Maintain a consistent arc length of 1.5–3 mm, ensure the filler rod is introduced from the side at a shallow angle and never brought into the arc column itself, and use HF non-contact arc start. If contamination occurs, break off the affected tungsten end and re-grind before continuing.

**Q: Do I need back purging when welding stainless steel pipe?**
Yes, for critical stainless steel pipe welds. The back (root) side of the weld is exposed to high temperatures and without inert gas protection will oxidise (a phenomenon called "sugaring"), destroying the corrosion resistance of the weld. Back-purge the pipe with pure Argon at 4–6 L/min through a fitting on the sealed end before and throughout the root pass welding.
