# Anvil

## Overview

The anvil is a massive, solid steel or cast-iron block used as a working surface for forging, hammering, bending, and shaping metal workpieces. It functions as a rigid reaction mass: because its mass is far greater than that of the workpiece and hammer, virtually all the kinetic energy of hammer blows is transferred into deforming the workpiece rather than being lost to elastic rebound of the anvil itself. The anvil is one of humanity's oldest metal-working tools, with designs largely unchanged for centuries, and remains an essential piece of equipment in any mechanical fabrication laboratory that includes blacksmithing, metalworking, or prototyping capabilities.

In an educational mechanical engineering lab, the anvil supports a wide range of forming and finishing operations. Students use it alongside hammers, punches, drifts, chisels, and swages to reshape heated or cold metal stock into brackets, hooks, hardware prototypes, artistic ironwork, and structural components. The anvil teaches fundamental engineering concepts such as plastic deformation, yield strength, work-hardening, thermal softening of metals (in hot work applications), and the lever action of forming tools. It bridges theoretical materials science with hands-on manufacturing practice.

A standard workshop anvil weighs between 50 and 200 kg and is mounted on a hardwood or steel stand at a height matched to the knuckle height of the smith (typically 750–850 mm from the floor). This ergonomic positioning maximizes the efficiency of hammer blows and reduces operator fatigue. The anvil is secured to its stand with U-bolts, chain, or lag screws to prevent it from moving or toppling during heavy hammering. Its hardened steel face provides a flat, durable work surface that resists deformation from repeated impact.

## Working Principle

The anvil functions on the principle of **Newton's third law** and **momentum conservation**. When a hammer strikes a workpiece resting on the anvil face, the impact force is transmitted through the workpiece into the anvil. Because the anvil's mass is many times greater than the hammer's, the anvil's velocity change (rebound) is negligible, and nearly all the impulse is transferred as a deforming force into the workpiece. This is the fundamental principle of inertial anvil function: mass and rigidity convert impact energy into plastic deformation.

The hardened **face** of the anvil (typically heat-treated to 50–55 HRC) must be harder than the material being worked so that the anvil does not deform under impact. However, it must not be so hard that it becomes brittle and chips. The **body** of a modern steel anvil is made from mild steel for toughness, while only the face plate is hardened tool steel. This composite construction balances hardness (wear resistance at the face) with toughness (impact resistance throughout the body).

When metal is heated above its **recrystallization temperature** (approximately 700–1,000 °C for mild steel, visible as orange-to-yellow heat), its yield strength drops dramatically, allowing large plastic deformations with relatively small hammer forces. Hot work on the anvil exploits this temperature-dependent plasticity. Cold work (working at room temperature) requires much greater force but produces work-hardened, dimensionally precise results. The anvil's **horn** (bick) allows curved work through its rounded profile, while the **hardy hole** and **pritchel hole** accommodate tooling that extends the anvil's functionality.

## Main Components

| Component | Function |
|---|---|
| Face (Work Surface) | Hardened, flat top surface where metal workpieces are shaped; must be kept smooth and free of deep marks |
| Horn (Bick) | Tapered conical projection at the front of the anvil; used for bending, curving, and forming rings and scrolls |
| Step (Cutting Table) | Slightly recessed shoulder between horn and face; used for chiselling and cutting to avoid damaging the hardened face |
| Hardy Hole (Square Hole) | Square socket (typically 25 mm) in the face that accepts hardy tooling such as hardy chisels, bending forks, and swages |
| Pritchel Hole (Round Hole) | Small round hole (~12–15 mm) in the face heel; provides clearance when punching holes through workpieces |
| Heel (Tail) | Far end of the anvil body, opposite the horn; provides a second flat work zone and houses the hardy and pritchel holes |
| Body / Waist | Central mass of the anvil below the face; provides inertia and structural continuity between face and base |
| Base (Foot) | Wide, stable bottom section of the anvil; bolted or chained to the stand for secure mounting |
| Anvil Stand | Hardwood or steel stand that supports the anvil at working height; absorbs vibration and prevents floor contact |
| Hardy Tooling Set | Interchangeable tools inserted in the hardy hole: hardy chisel, bending fork, bottom swage, and set hammer |

## Inputs and Outputs

### Inputs
- **Workpiece metal** — Mild steel bar, rod, flat bar, or sheet stock (heated or cold); occasionally copper, aluminium, or bronze for cold work
- **Heat (for hot work)** — Propane or coal forge heat, raising mild steel to forging temperature (900–1,200 °C / orange to yellow heat)
- **Hammer energy** — Kinetic energy from hand hammer (ball-peen, cross-peen, or rounding hammer), power hammer, or pneumatic hammer
- **Hardy and accessory tooling** — Chisels, swages, fullers, drifts, and punches inserted in the hardy hole or applied to the workpiece
- **Quench medium (optional)** — Water or oil for quenching hot work after forming to set shape or begin heat treatment

### Outputs
- **Formed metal workpiece** — Bent, drawn, upset, punched, or shaped component matching the target geometry
- **Scale (mill scale)** — Oxidised surface layer that flakes off during hot forging; a normal byproduct of high-temperature work
- **Swarf and offcuts** — Material removed by chiselling or cutting over the step or hardy chisel
- **Noise** — Impact noise from hammer blows, typically 90–120 dB(A) peak; hearing protection is mandatory
- **Heat** — Radiated heat from the hot workpiece and from the forge (if adjacent); ambient temperature rise in the lab

## Operating Procedure

1. **Inspect the anvil and stand** — Verify the anvil is securely mounted to its stand with no wobble or movement. Confirm the face is free of deep gouges, cracks, or chipping. Check the stand for stability.
2. **Prepare PPE** — Don heat-resistant leather gloves (for hot work), leather apron, safety glasses or face shield, hearing protection (earplugs or earmuffs rated for 30+ dB reduction), and steel-toed boots.
3. **Heat the workpiece (for hot work)** — Place the workpiece in the forge and bring it to the correct forging temperature: bright orange (900–1,050 °C) for drawing and bending, yellow-white (1,100–1,250 °C) for heavy upsetting. Do not work steel below cherry red (<700 °C) as it becomes brittle (blue-shortness risk).
4. **Remove the workpiece from the forge** — Use forge tongs to grip the workpiece securely, with jaws matched to the stock cross-section. Carry the workpiece directly to the anvil without delay.
5. **Position the workpiece on the anvil** — Place the workpiece on the correct anvil zone: flat face for general work, horn for curves, step for cutting.
6. **Strike decisive, accurate blows** — Use the hammer with controlled, rhythmic blows aligned with the intended deformation direction. Flat face of the hammer square to the workpiece surface to avoid unwanted marks.
7. **Rotate and reposition the workpiece as needed** — Between blows, reposition the workpiece to work the entire affected area uniformly. Use the tongs to turn, flip, or slide the stock.
8. **Monitor the working temperature** — Stop hammering when the steel cools to black heat (below 700 °C / dark red). Return it to the forge for reheating. Working cold steel causes internal cracking.
9. **Use hardy tools as required** — Insert the appropriate hardy tool into the hardy hole and work the hot metal over it (e.g., use the fuller to draw out stock, the hardy chisel to cut off).
10. **Quench or air-cool as required** — After the final heat, either quench the workpiece in water (for rapid cooling and some hardening), oil (for controlled martensitic hardening of tool steels), or allow air cooling for mild steel.
11. **Inspect the finished workpiece** — Check dimensions, straightness, surface quality, and geometry against the target drawing using rulers, squares, and calipers.
12. **Clean the anvil face** — Brush scale, swarf, and debris from the face and hardy hole after each session. Do not leave hot workpieces resting on the anvil face for extended periods.

## Safety Rules

1. **Always wear hearing protection** — Anvil hammer strikes produce impulse noise of 95–120 dB(A); cumulative exposure without protection causes permanent hearing loss.
2. **Wear a leather apron and heat-resistant gloves during hot work** — Radiated heat from the workpiece and flying scale can cause burns to the hands, arms, and torso.
3. **Never strike a cold anvil face with a hardened hammer on a cold day** — In cold conditions, the hardened face and hammer can shatter, sending fragments at lethal velocity. Warm the anvil face gently with the hot workpiece first.
4. **Never hammer a workpiece below dark cherry red (700 °C) during hot work** — Cold-hammering steel in the blue-shortness range (230–370 °C) causes internal cracking; working below cherry red in hot work causes the same effect.
5. **Use correct tong selection** — Tongs must fit the stock cross-section securely; loose tongs allow the hot workpiece to be dropped, causing severe burns to operators and bystanders.
6. **Maintain a clear work zone around the anvil** — Bystanders must stay at least 2 m from the anvil during hammering to avoid injury from rebound scale, flying swarf, or a misdirected hammer blow.
7. **Do not place hardened tools (chisels, dies) directly on the anvil face and strike them cold** — Hardened tool-on-hardened face impacts can cause chipping and fragmentation; always use a softer pad or work the tool hot.
8. **Secure the workpiece with tongs at all times during hot work** — Never hold hot workpieces by hand, even momentarily; heat transfer through gloves still causes burns within seconds.
9. **Wear eye protection during all anvil work** — Scale, swarf, and hammer burrs can be ejected at high velocity from the impact zone; safety glasses or face shield are mandatory.
10. **Never use the horn to straighten by striking downward on it** — The horn is not designed for direct downward hammer blows from above; this can crack or deform the horn.
11. **Ensure the forge and anvil area are well-ventilated** — Hot forging produces scale smoke and combustion gases; a local exhaust ventilation (LEV) system or open-door ventilation is required.
12. **Inspect hammer handles before each session** — Loose, cracked, or splintered handles cause hammer heads to fly off during swings; replace damaged handles immediately.

## Common Applications

- **Drawing out** — Reducing cross-section and increasing length of bar stock for hooks, pins, and structural members
- **Bending** — Forming right angles, curves, and scrolls in flat bar and rod over the horn or face edge
- **Upsetting** — Increasing cross-section (shortening) of a bar to form bolt heads, flanges, and collars
- **Punching** — Driving a punch through hot stock over the pritchel hole to create holes without material removal
- **Cutting / Hardie work** — Using the hardy chisel to cut off stock or split material over the step
- **Riveting** — Forming hot or cold rivet heads over the anvil face in structural lap joint assembly
- **Straightening** — Removing bends and twists from bar and rod stock by targeted hammer blows on the flat face
- **Prototype hardware fabrication** — Creating custom brackets, hinges, fasteners, and tooling components for lab projects

## Maintenance

- **After each use:** Brush scale and debris from the face, horn, hardy hole, and pritchel hole with a stiff wire brush.
- **Weekly:** Inspect the face for deep dings or gouges from incorrect hammer strikes; minor marks can be dressed with a flat file or surface grinder by a qualified technician.
- **Weekly:** Check the stand mounting bolts (U-bolts, lag screws, or chain) for tightness; heavy hammering loosens fasteners over time.
- **Monthly:** Apply a light coat of linseed oil or WD-40 to the face if the anvil will be idle for more than a few days to prevent rust.
- **Monthly:** Inspect the horn for cracks or surface pitting; report any cracks immediately as horn fracture under impact is dangerous.
- **Monthly:** Check that the anvil stand is structurally sound — no cracked welds, split wood, or damaged legs.
- **As needed:** Re-dress the face with a surface grinder if deep depressions or raised burrs develop; this work must be done by a qualified technician familiar with hardened steel grinding.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What is the correct working temperature for mild steel on the anvil?**
A: Mild steel should be worked in the bright orange to yellow-white range (900–1,250 °C). A bright orange heat is ideal for most bending and drawing operations. Yellow-white is used for heavy upsetting. Never work below dark cherry red (approximately 700 °C), as the steel becomes brittle in this range and will develop internal cracks.

**Q: Why is the anvil face slightly convex — is mine damaged?**
A: Many traditional anvils have a very slightly convex (crowned) face, usually less than 0.5 mm across the full face width. This is intentional — it prevents the hammer from creating "fish-scale" edge marks on the workpiece by ensuring the centre of the hammer contacts the workpiece before the edge does. A highly convex or uneven face, however, indicates wear and should be assessed by a technician.

**Q: Can I weld on the anvil or use it as a welding table?**
A: No. Do not use the hardened anvil face as a welding fixture or ground the welding circuit through it. Stray welding current passing through hardened steel can cause hydrogen embrittlement and localized annealing, softening or cracking the face. Use a dedicated welding table or steel plate for welding work.

**Q: How do I know what size hammer to use with the anvil?**
A: Hammer weight should be matched to the task. A 0.5–1 kg ball-peen hammer suits light shaping, riveting, and cold work. A 1–2 kg cross-peen or rounding hammer suits general hot forging on bar stock up to 25 mm. A 3–5 kg sledgehammer (used two-handed or with a striker) is reserved for heavy upsetting and large stock. The anvil mass should be roughly 50× the hammer mass for efficient energy transfer.

**Q: Why does my anvil ring loudly? Is this normal?**
A: All anvils ring to some degree from the elastic vibration of the steel body. Excessive ringing (a long, high-pitched tone) indicates the anvil is well-mounted and of good quality (high carbon steel), but is also a hearing hazard. Ringing can be reduced by placing a chain or magnetic rubber sheet around the waist/base, which damps the vibration. Hearing protection remains mandatory regardless.

## Related Machines

- **Propane / Coal Forge** — Heats metal workpieces to forging temperature for hot work on the anvil
- **Post Vise (Leg Vise)** — Heavy forging vise mounted adjacent to the anvil; holds workpieces for bending and filing
- **Power Hammer / Pneumatic Hammer** — Machine-powered hammer for high-volume or high-force forging operations beyond hand-hammer capability
- **Bench Grinder** — Used for tool sharpening (chisels, punches) and dressing burrs from forged workpieces
- **Angle Grinder** — Used for scale removal, surface finishing, and cutting of forged metal pieces
- **MIG / TIG Welder** — Used to join or repair forged components after shaping on the anvil
- **Spot Machine (Resistance Spot Welder)** — Alternative metal joining method for sheet-metal components in the lab
