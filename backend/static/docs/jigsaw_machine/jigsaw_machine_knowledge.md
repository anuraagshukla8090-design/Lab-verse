# Jigsaw Machine

## Overview

A jigsaw is a handheld or bench-mounted power tool that uses a short, fine-toothed blade reciprocating in a vertical plane to cut curved, straight, or irregular shapes in a wide range of materials including wood, plywood, MDF, plastics, sheet metal, and ceramics. Unlike stationary saws, the jigsaw is a portable tool that is brought to the workpiece rather than the workpiece being fed into a stationary machine, giving it exceptional flexibility for cuts that cannot be reached by a fixed machine. The blade extends below the base plate (shoe), which rests flat on the workpiece surface and acts as a depth-setting and angle-referencing plane.

In a mechanical engineering laboratory, the jigsaw is a versatile and frequently used tool for rough-shaping panels, cutting large sheet material that cannot be easily moved to a stationary saw, making plunge cuts into the interior of a sheet, and producing curved cuts in workpieces of moderate thickness. Students learn to handle a powered cutting tool safely, set up workholding for portable operations, and manage the forces imposed by the reciprocating blade — skills that are directly transferable to a broad range of fabrication and site-work contexts.

The jigsaw's orbital action feature, available on most modern models, moves the blade in a slight forward arc on the cutting stroke in addition to the up-and-down motion, increasing material removal rate in wood and soft materials while keeping the surface finish acceptable. Selecting the correct blade (tooth pitch, blade material, tooth geometry) for the target workpiece material is a fundamental skill the machine teaches and one that has direct parallels in all other cutting operations in the laboratory.

## Working Principle

The jigsaw converts rotary motor motion to reciprocating linear blade motion through an eccentric crank mechanism. The motor drives a gear that rotates an eccentric pin, which drives the blade holder vertically. The stroke length is fixed (typically 16–26 mm) and the number of strokes per minute (SPM) is controlled by a variable-speed trigger or dial, typically ranging from 500 to 3,100 SPM.

The blade cuts on the upstroke in standard configuration; each tooth shears a small chip from the material as it moves upward through the kerf. This means the clean face of the cut is on the bottom surface of the workpiece. Operators who require a clean top surface (e.g., when cutting veneered panels) should cut face-down or use a reverse-tooth (down-cutting) blade.

Orbital action, when engaged, introduces a small forward (horizontal) movement of the blade on the upstroke, making the blade behave more like a jab at the material rather than a pure vertical slice. This increases the chip size and cutting speed significantly — up to four orbital action modes are typically provided (0 = none, 1–3 = increasing aggressiveness). Orbital action is suitable for wood, plastics, and soft materials but should be turned off (set to 0) when cutting metal to prevent blade breakage and to maintain accuracy.

The base plate can be tilted up to 45° left or right relative to the blade, allowing bevel cuts. A blade guide roller behind the blade provides lateral support to reduce deflection during straight or gently curved cuts.

## Inputs and Outputs

### Inputs
- Workpiece material: wood, plywood, MDF, plastics, sheet metal, ceramic tile
- Electrical power: 110–240 V AC, 50/60 Hz; or 18–20 V DC (battery-powered models)
- Correct jigsaw blade matched to material (wood, metal, plastics, ceramics)
- Speed setting (SPM) appropriate for the material
- Orbital action setting (0–3) appropriate for the material
- Bevel angle setting of the base plate

### Outputs
- Workpiece cut to the desired straight, curved, or bevel profile
- Fine sawdust or metal chips
- Off-cut material
- Noise and vibration during operation

## Operating Procedure

1. **Inspect the jigsaw** — check that the blade clamp is secure, the blade is undamaged, the shoe is flat and undamaged, and the power cord is intact.
2. **Select the correct blade** — choose the appropriate blade for the material: coarse tooth (lower TPI) for wood, fine tooth (high TPI) for metal and plastics, specialty blades for ceramics.
3. **Put on PPE** — wear safety glasses, hearing protection, and dust protection (P2 mask) when cutting dusty materials such as MDF.
4. **Secure the workpiece** — clamp the workpiece to a bench or sawhorse so it is stable and the cut line overhangs a clear space; the blade must not contact the support surface.
5. **Mark the cut line** — clearly mark the cut path on the workpiece with a pencil, marker, or scribe; for straight cuts, clamp a straightedge guide.
6. **Set speed and orbital action** — select the appropriate SPM and orbital action level for the material.
7. **Set the bevel angle** — loosen the bevel lock, tilt the shoe to the required bevel angle (if required), and lock it firmly.
8. **Position the shoe on the workpiece** — place the front of the shoe flat against the workpiece, with the blade in free air, before starting the motor.
9. **Start the motor and allow it to reach speed** — engage the trigger and wait for full speed before contacting the workpiece.
10. **Begin the cut** — advance the jigsaw forward gently, keeping the shoe flat on the workpiece and following the cut line.
11. **Maintain steady, even feed rate** — do not push the saw faster than the blade can cut; allow the blade to do the work.
12. **For plunge cuts** — tilt the saw forward on the toe of the shoe, start the motor, and slowly lower the spinning blade into the surface until the shoe is flat.
13. **Complete the cut** — continue until the cut is complete; support both sections of the workpiece near the cut end to prevent the piece splitting.
14. **Release the trigger and set the saw down safely** — ensure the blade has stopped before setting the saw on a surface.

## Safety Rules

1. **Always clamp the workpiece firmly** before cutting — a workpiece that shifts under a jigsaw is extremely hazardous.
2. **Keep both hands on the tool** at all times during cutting — one hand on the body handle, one on the front knob (if fitted); never guide the workpiece with the free hand near the blade.
3. **Never start the blade while it is in contact with the workpiece** — always start with the blade clear, then advance into the cut.
4. **Ensure the power cord clears the blade path** before cutting — dragging or cutting through the cord is a severe electrical hazard.
5. **Wear safety glasses at all times** — chips and fragments are ejected upward by the blade on every stroke.
6. **Do not cut material above the rated capacity** of the blade or machine; forcing through thick or hard material causes blade breakage and motor overload.
7. **Use the correct blade for the material** — a wood blade used on metal will strip its teeth immediately and may break, causing blade ejection.
8. **Never reach below the workpiece** during cutting — the blade tip protrudes below and reciprocates at high speed.
9. **Wait for the blade to stop completely** before setting the saw down, changing blades, or examining the workpiece.
10. **Do not use a damaged or bent blade** — a deformed blade vibrates excessively and will break during use.
11. **Disconnect from power before changing blades** — accidental trigger engagement during blade changes is a common workshop injury cause.
12. **Keep the work area clear** of shavings, cords, and obstacles to allow safe movement of both the operator and the saw.

## Common Applications

- Cutting curved and straight profiles in plywood and timber panels for structural model fabrication
- Cutting large sheet metal panels where a stationary saw is impractical
- Making plunge cuts into sheet material for access openings, switches, and connector panels
- Rough-shaping workpieces prior to finish machining on a milling machine or surface grinder
- Cutting pipe insulation, gasket material, and foam pads to shape
- Cutting fibre-reinforced plastic (FRP) and composite sheet for lightweight structural components
- Trimming site-assembled structures and fixtures to final dimension
- Cutting countertop and panel openings for laboratory installation projects

## Maintenance

- **Inspect and replace blades** when dull, bent, or missing teeth; a new blade for every significant job is good practice.
- **Clean the shoe base plate** after each session — sawdust and metal filings trapped under the shoe cause scratching and inaccurate cuts.
- **Lubricate the blade holder reciprocating mechanism** with a small amount of light oil through the service port every 50 hours.
- **Check the blade clamp spring or locking mechanism** for positive retention; a worn clamp allows the blade to be pulled out during cutting.
- **Inspect the power cord** at the strain relief and along its length for cuts, kinks, or abrasion; replace a damaged cord before further use.
- **Blow out motor vents** with compressed air every 20–30 hours to prevent dust accumulation and overheating.
- **Check motor carbon brushes** every 6 months on brush-type motors; replace brushes when worn to 6 mm.
- **Test the orbital action mechanism** for smooth engagement between all four settings; stiff selectors indicate internal contamination requiring service.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Why does my jigsaw leave a rough, splintered edge on plywood?**
Splintering on the top face occurs because jigsaw blades typically cut on the upstroke, and the upward blade motion tears the top veneer fibres rather than shearing them cleanly. To get a clean top surface, cut with the good face down, use a fine-tooth blade (≥10 TPI for wood), apply masking tape over the cut line on the good face, or use a down-cutting (reverse tooth) blade.

**Q: The saw is cutting in a curve even though I'm following a straight line — why?**
Blade drift occurs when the blade is dull and the operator applies side pressure to compensate, or when orbital action is set too high and causing the flexible blade to wander. Replace the blade, reduce orbital action to 0 or 1, and use a fence or straightedge guide clamped to the workpiece. Do not push the saw faster than the blade's natural cutting rate.

**Q: Can I cut stainless steel with a jigsaw?**
Yes, with a bi-metal jigsaw blade rated for stainless steel, a slow speed setting (600–1,200 SPM), and orbital action set to 0. Apply cutting oil to the blade and advance very slowly. Stainless work-hardens if the blade rubs without cutting, which will rapidly destroy the blade. Clamp the sheet firmly to prevent vibration, which causes premature blade failure.

**Q: How do I make a perfectly straight cut with a jigsaw?**
Clamp a straight piece of timber or a proprietary jigsaw fence parallel to the cut line at a distance equal to the offset between the blade and the edge of the shoe. Run the edge of the shoe against this guide throughout the cut. For production runs, use a commercially available parallel fence that attaches to the shoe.
