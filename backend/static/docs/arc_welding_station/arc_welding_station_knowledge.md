# Arc Welding Station

## Overview

The Arc Welding Station is a dedicated workspace equipped with a Shielded Metal Arc Welding (SMAW) power source, electrode holder, ground clamp, welding table, and full personal protective equipment. Also known as "stick welding," arc welding is one of the oldest and most widely used fusion welding processes, making it an essential skill taught in mechanical and manufacturing engineering laboratories worldwide.

In an educational mechanical lab, the arc welding station introduces students to the fundamental concepts of electric arc formation, heat generation, metallurgical bonding, and filler metal deposition. Students learn to control heat input, travel speed, and electrode angle — core competencies that underpin virtually every other welding process. The station is intentionally designed to be approachable with minimal setup while still presenting the full complexity of weld pool management.

The work enabled at this station spans from simple butt joints on mild steel plate to practice with fillet welds, lap joints, and T-joints. Students gain hands-on experience interpreting weld defects, adjusting amperage, and selecting appropriate electrodes for different base metals. These skills transfer directly to industry contexts in structural fabrication, pipeline welding, repair work, and heavy equipment manufacturing.

## Working Principle

Arc welding operates on the principle of sustained electrical discharge between a consumable electrode and the base metal workpiece. The welding machine — typically a transformer-rectifier or inverter-based power source — produces a high-amperage, low-voltage DC or AC current. When the electrode tip is brought into contact with the workpiece and then withdrawn slightly, an electric arc forms in the gap. Arc temperatures reach 3,500–6,500 °C, sufficient to melt both the base metal and the electrode core.

The electrode is coated with a flux compound that serves two purposes: it generates a shielding gas (CO₂, CO, and other gases) through thermal decomposition, protecting the molten weld pool from atmospheric oxygen and nitrogen; and it forms a slag layer over the solidifying bead, which further shields the weld and controls cooling rate. The electrode core melts progressively, depositing filler metal into the joint.

The key engineering concepts governing this process include Ohm's Law (V = IR), Joule heating (Q = I²Rt), and solidification metallurgy. Amperage controls heat input and penetration depth; too high causes burn-through, too low causes lack of fusion. The welder must coordinate electrode angle, arc length, and travel speed simultaneously to produce a sound weld bead.

## Main Components

| Component | Function |
|---|---|
| Welding Power Source (Transformer/Inverter) | Converts mains AC power to controlled high-amperage welding current (AC or DC) |
| Electrode Holder (Stinger) | Insulated clamp that secures the consumable electrode and carries welding current to it |
| Ground Clamp (Work Clamp) | Connects the workpiece to the negative terminal of the circuit, completing the welding circuit |
| Welding Electrodes (Rods) | Consumable flux-coated metal rods that serve as both filler metal and arc sustainer |
| Welding Table | Heavy steel work surface that provides a stable, grounded platform for the workpiece |
| Amperage / Polarity Selector | Controls on the power source for setting current level and DC polarity (DCEP/DCEN) |
| Welding Helmet (Auto-Darkening) | Protects eyes and face from UV/IR radiation and arc flash; darkens automatically on arc strike |
| Chipping Hammer & Wire Brush | Used to remove slag from the completed weld bead for inspection |
| Ventilation / Fume Extractor | Removes hazardous welding fumes and particulates from the operator's breathing zone |
| Welding Curtains / Screens | Opaque barriers that protect nearby personnel from arc flash and UV radiation |

## Inputs and Outputs

### Inputs
- Base metal workpieces (typically mild steel, low-alloy steel, or cast iron)
- Consumable flux-coated electrodes (e.g., E6013, E6011, E7018) of appropriate diameter
- Electrical power (single-phase or three-phase mains supply, 230 V / 415 V)
- Operator-set parameters: amperage, polarity (DCEP or DCEN), electrode angle, travel speed
- Joint preparation: cleaned, tack-welded, or fixtured workpieces

### Outputs
- Fused weld bead joining base metal pieces
- Slag layer (removed by chipping hammer post-weld)
- Welding fumes and gases (extracted by ventilation system)
- Spatter (molten metal droplets on surrounding surface)
- Heat-affected zone (HAZ) in the base metal adjacent to the weld
- Electrode stub (consumed electrode remnant)

## Operating Procedure

1. **Inspect the station** before use: check cables for damage, verify ground clamp is secure, confirm the power source is off.
2. **Select the correct electrode** based on the base metal, joint type, and position. Check electrode diameter matches the material thickness (rule of thumb: 1 mm electrode per mm of plate thickness up to 6 mm).
3. **Set amperage** on the power source according to the electrode manufacturer's recommendation (typically stamped on the box). Set polarity as required (E7018: DCEP; E6013: AC or DCEP).
4. **Prepare the workpiece**: clean the joint area with an angle grinder or wire brush to remove mill scale, rust, paint, and moisture. Tack-weld the joint to maintain alignment.
5. **Position the workpiece** securely on the welding table. Attach the ground clamp as close to the weld zone as practical.
6. **Don all PPE**: welding helmet (shade 10–12), leather gloves, leather apron, safety boots, and fire-resistant jacket. Ensure ventilation or fume extractor is running.
7. **Load the electrode** into the electrode holder at the correct angle.
8. **Strike the arc** by either scratching or tapping the electrode against the base metal, then immediately establishing the correct arc length (approximately equal to the electrode diameter).
9. **Maintain arc length and travel angle**: keep a consistent arc length (3–5 mm), hold the electrode at 60–75° from horizontal in the direction of travel, and advance at a steady speed.
10. **Complete the weld pass**: move uniformly along the joint without stopping. Use weave or stringer bead technique as required.
11. **Allow the weld to cool** for at least 30 seconds. Do not quench with water unless specifically required.
12. **Remove slag** using the chipping hammer and wire brush. Wear safety glasses during this step.
13. **Inspect the weld** visually for cracks, porosity, undercut, overlap, and incomplete fusion.
14. **Log the weld parameters** (amperage, electrode type, base metal) in the lab record sheet.
15. **Power off the machine**, hang the electrode holder safely, and clean the work area.

## Safety Rules

1. **Always wear a welding helmet with the correct shade lens** (minimum shade 10 for SMAW) before striking an arc. Never look at the arc with unprotected eyes.
2. **Never weld in a wet environment** or with wet gloves and clothing; electrical shock hazard is severe at welding currents.
3. **Keep flammable materials at least 10 metres away** from the welding area. Have a fire extinguisher (CO₂ or dry powder) within arm's reach.
4. **Inspect electrode holder and cables before each use**: damaged insulation must be repaired or replaced before welding begins.
5. **Always connect the ground clamp before picking up the electrode holder** to avoid accidental arc strikes on unintended surfaces.
6. **Ensure adequate ventilation or a fume extractor is active** before beginning. Never weld galvanised, lead-painted, or cadmium-coated metal without approved respiratory protection (P100 respirator minimum).
7. **Never change electrodes with bare hands**: the stub and holder are electrically live; use insulated gloves and point the stinger away from your body when changing.
8. **Post warning signs and deploy welding screens** to protect anyone working nearby from arc flash and UV exposure.
9. **Do not leave a hot workpiece unattended** without placing a "HOT METAL" marker. Allow adequate cooling before handling.
10. **Keep the welding area dry and clear of clutter**, especially flammable dust, oil rags, and paper.
11. **Do not weld containers or pipes** that have held flammable liquids without following confined-space and hot-work permit procedures.
12. **Disconnect power before performing any maintenance** on cables, connections, or the power source.

## Maintenance

- **Daily**: Inspect electrode holder jaws, cable connections, and ground clamp for wear or burning; wipe spatter off the welding table.
- **Weekly**: Check and tighten all cable terminal connections; inspect insulation on all leads for cracking or abrasion.
- **Monthly**: Clean internal fan filters on the power source unit; check duty cycle indicator for abnormal behaviour.
- **As needed**: Replace worn electrode holder jaws when grip becomes unreliable; replace ground clamp when contact area is pitted or corroded.
- **Periodically**: Have a qualified electrician test insulation resistance of the primary circuit and verify earthing continuity.
- **Consumables**: Keep adequate stock of common electrodes (E6013, E7018) in a dry, heated rod oven or sealed container to prevent moisture absorption.
- **Record keeping**: Log all machine faults, maintenance actions, and amperage calibration checks in the station logbook.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Why does my arc keep extinguishing?**
The most common causes are amperage set too low, a damp or contaminated electrode, or too long an arc length. Increase the amperage by 5–10 A increments, ensure the electrode was stored in a dry rod oven, and keep the arc length approximately equal to the electrode diameter.

**Q: What does DCEP and DCEN mean, and when do I use each?**
DCEP (Direct Current Electrode Positive) directs more heat into the electrode, producing deeper penetration and a smoother arc — used with most structural electrodes like E7018. DCEN (Direct Current Electrode Negative) puts more heat into the workpiece, giving faster deposition with less penetration — used for thin material and some surfacing electrodes. Always follow the electrode manufacturer's polarity recommendation.

**Q: Why is there porosity in my weld?**
Porosity (gas pockets) is typically caused by: moisture in the electrode coating (store electrodes in a rod oven at 120 °C), contamination on the base metal (grease, paint, rust), or too short an arc that traps gas. Clean the joint thoroughly and use a fresh, dry electrode.

**Q: How do I control weld distortion?**
Distortion occurs because weld metal contracts on cooling. Minimise it by using proper joint fit-up, tack welding at frequent intervals, using a backstep welding sequence, balancing welds on both sides of a joint, and clamping or fixturing the workpiece before welding.

**Q: What shade lens do I need in my welding helmet?**
For SMAW, a minimum shade 10 is recommended for currents up to 200 A; shade 11 or 12 is appropriate for currents above 200 A. Many modern auto-darkening helmets allow adjustable shade from 9 to 13 and are the recommended choice for educational labs.
