# Gas Welding Station

## Overview

The Gas Welding Station is a workstation equipped with oxy-acetylene (or oxy-LPG) cutting and welding equipment, including high-pressure gas cylinders, dual-stage regulators, reinforced hoses, a welding/cutting torch with interchangeable tips, a welding table, and full personal protective equipment. Oxy-fuel welding is one of the oldest thermal joining processes, dating to the early twentieth century, and remains an essential educational topic in mechanical engineering programs due to its fundamental role in the history of fabrication and its continued use in maintenance, brazing, silver soldering, and cutting applications.

In an educational lab, the gas welding station teaches students the physics of combustion chemistry, flame adjustment, heat conduction in metals, and manual filler rod technique without reliance on electricity. Students learn to identify the three types of oxy-acetylene flame — carburising, neutral, and oxidising — and understand how each affects the weld metallurgy. The deliberate pace of the gas welding process compared to electric arc processes gives students more time to observe and understand the weld pool behaviour.

The station enables a broad range of thermal metalworking tasks beyond fusion welding: oxy-fuel cutting (using a cutting attachment), brazing (joining dissimilar metals with a bronze filler below base metal melting point), silver soldering (for precision copper, brass, and thin steel work), metal heating and bending, flame hardening, and heat-straightening of distorted structural members. This versatility makes it a uniquely multi-purpose station in any mechanical fabrication laboratory.

## Working Principle

Oxy-acetylene welding combines acetylene (C₂H₂) and oxygen (O₂) in a welding tip to produce a flame reaching approximately 3,100–3,500 °C — the highest temperature achievable with a gas flame and sufficient to melt steel, aluminium, brass, and copper. The combustion reaction occurs in two stages: the inner cone (primary zone) burns acetylene with oxygen in a near-stoichiometric ratio, producing carbon monoxide (CO) and hydrogen (H₂); the outer flame envelope (secondary zone) combusts these products with atmospheric air to produce CO₂ and H₂O.

The flame type is controlled entirely by the oxygen-to-acetylene ratio at the torch valves:
- **Carburising flame** (excess acetylene): produces a feathery white inner cone; used for hard-facing and welding high-carbon steels.
- **Neutral flame** (balanced ratio): inner cone well-defined, no feather; used for most fusion welding of mild steel, aluminium, and copper.
- **Oxidising flame** (excess oxygen): inner cone shorter and pointed, hissing sound; used for brass and bronze welding where a slight oxidising atmosphere prevents zinc fuming.

Heat is transferred to the base metal by convection and radiation from the flame. Unlike arc welding, there is no electrical circuit; the welder controls heat input entirely through torch-to-work distance, tip size, gas flow, and torch movement pattern. Filler rod is manually dipped into the leading edge of the molten pool as in TIG welding.

## Main Components

| Component | Function |

| Oxygen Cylinder | High-pressure cylinder (up to 200 bar) storing compressed oxygen for combustion support |
| Dual-Stage Regulators | Reduce cylinder pressure to safe working pressure (acetylene: ≤ 100 kPa; oxygen: up to 400 kPa) |
| Reinforced Rubber Hoses | Deliver fuel and oxygen from regulators to the torch (red = acetylene/fuel, blue/green = oxygen) |
| Welding Torch Body | Mixing chamber where acetylene and oxygen combine before combustion at the tip |
| Interchangeable Welding Tips | Threaded copper tips of varying orifice size, controlling flame size and heat input |
| Cutting Attachment | Torch head attachment with a central high-pressure oxygen jet for oxy-fuel cutting |
| Flashback Arrestors | Inline safety valves on each hose fitting that prevent flame from travelling back into the hoses or cylinders |
| Non-Return Check Valves | Prevent backflow of gas into the opposite gas supply line |
| Welding Table & Firebricks | Refractory surface that withstands flame impingement and supports heated workpieces |

## Inputs and Outputs

### Inputs
- Acetylene gas (working pressure: 70–100 kPa; never exceed 100 kPa)
- Oxygen gas (working pressure: 200–400 kPa depending on tip size)
- Base metal workpieces (mild steel, copper, brass, aluminium)
- Filler rods (matched to base metal: mild steel, bronze, aluminium, silver alloy for brazing)
- Flux (for aluminium, cast iron, and stainless steel welding; prevents oxidation)
- Operator parameters: oxygen/acetylene ratio (flame type), torch-to-work distance, travel speed, filler addition rate

### Outputs
- Fused weld bead or brazed joint
- Combustion products: CO₂, H₂O vapour
- Trace CO and unburnt hydrocarbons (require ventilation)
- Metal oxide scale and flux residue (must be cleaned after welding aluminium/cast iron)
- Heat-affected zone (wider than arc welding due to diffuse heat source)
- Spent filler rod stubs

## Operating Procedure

1. **Check cylinder contents and locations**: confirm both cylinders are chained upright. Check oxygen and acetylene pressures on the cylinder gauges.
2. **Inspect hoses and connections**: check for cracks, crimps, or loose fittings. Verify flashback arrestors are installed on both hoses at the regulator and torch ends.
3. **Open oxygen cylinder valve slowly** (anti-clockwise), then fully. Turn in the regulator adjusting screw to set working pressure (e.g., 200 kPa for a No. 3 tip).
4. **Open acetylene cylinder valve slowly** — no more than 1.5 turns (never fully open an acetylene cylinder). Set working pressure to 70 kPa; never exceed 100 kPa.
5. **Purge each hose separately**: open the torch oxygen valve for 2–3 seconds to purge the oxygen hose, then close. Repeat for the acetylene valve. This removes any air or contamination.
6. **Don all PPE**: welding goggles (shade 5 for gas welding, shade 4–5 for brazing), leather gloves, leather apron, and safety boots. Ensure the area is ventilated and all flammable materials are removed from the vicinity.
7. **Light the torch**: open the acetylene torch valve 1/2 turn and ignite with a friction lighter (never a cigarette lighter or matches). The flame will be sooty and yellow.
8. **Adjust to a neutral flame**: slowly open the oxygen torch valve until the sooty yellow flame transforms: a distinct, bright inner cone forms and the feathery carburising envelope disappears. This is the neutral flame.
9. **Adjust for the task**: for mild steel welding, use a neutral flame. Adjust to a slight carburising or oxidising flame as the specific material requires.
10. **Preheat the joint**: bring the torch to 45–60° and play the flame across the joint area to bring the base metal to dull red heat before beginning to deposit filler.
11. **Establish and advance the weld pool**: hold the inner cone tip 3–5 mm from the metal surface. When a pool forms, add filler rod at 30–40° by dipping the tip into the leading edge of the pool, then advance the torch.
12. **Complete the weld and withdraw the flame**: at the end of the joint, continue adding filler to fill the crater, then withdraw the torch progressively to reduce heat input before extinguishing.
13. **Shut down the torch**: close the acetylene valve first, then the oxygen valve. This prevents a backfire and avoids a sooty deposit on the tip.
14. **Close cylinder valves**: turn both cylinder valves clockwise to close. Reopen the torch valves to bleed gas from the hoses and regulators. Close torch valves. Back off the regulator adjusting screws.
15. **Allow the workpiece to cool** on the firebrick and place a HOT METAL warning marker before leaving the area.

## Safety Rules

1. **Never exceed 100 kPa (15 psi) acetylene pressure**: above this pressure, acetylene becomes chemically unstable and can decompose explosively, even without oxygen present.
2. **Always install flashback arrestors on both hoses** at both the regulator and the torch ends. Never operate the station without arrestors; a flashback can cause catastrophic cylinder explosion.
3. **Use only an approved friction lighter** to ignite the torch. Never use matches or cigarette lighters, which require the hand to be positioned near the torch tip during ignition.
4. **Open acetylene cylinder valve no more than 1.5 turns** to allow rapid closure in an emergency. The oxygen cylinder valve should be opened fully to prevent pressure drop across the packing.
5. **Keep acetylene and oxygen cylinders at least 6 metres apart** when not connected, or separated by a non-combustible wall at least 1.5 m high, to prevent simultaneous involvement in a fire.
6. **Never leave a lit torch unattended**, even for a moment. If you must leave the station, extinguish the flame following the proper shutdown sequence.
7. **Keep oil and grease away from oxygen equipment**: oxygen under pressure can ignite hydrocarbon lubricants violently. Never use oil to lubricate cylinder valves or regulators.
8. **Operate with full ventilation**: combustion consumes oxygen and produces CO₂ and trace CO. Welding copper, brass, and zinc-coated metals releases toxic fumes requiring extraction.
9. **Position cylinders upright and secured** at all times. If a cylinder falls and the valve breaks off, the cylinder can become a projectile capable of penetrating concrete walls.
10. **Wear correct-shade welding goggles**: gas welding requires shade 4–5 goggles, NOT arc welding helmets. Arc helmets shade out the soft blue-green gas flame, making weld pool visibility poor.
11. **Never use acetylene cylinders that have become overheated** (too hot to touch comfortably). If a cylinder becomes hot in a fire, evacuate the area and cool with water from a distance.
12. **Conduct a leak test before each use**: brush soapy water around all connections and hose fittings with gas flowing; bubbles indicate a leak that must be fixed before use.

## Common Applications

- Fusion welding of mild steel plate (butt and fillet joints) for foundational weld pool practice
- Oxy-fuel cutting of mild steel using the cutting attachment
- Bronze brazing of copper pipe fittings (plumbing fabrication exercises)
- Silver soldering of precision instrument components and electrical conductors
- Flame straightening and heat-bending of steel sections and bars
- Aluminium welding with flux (used where TIG is unavailable)
- Hard-facing and surfacing of wear components using bronze or hard-alloy rods
- Heating for shrink-fit assembly and disassembly of press-fit machine components

## Maintenance

- **After each use**: Inspect welding tips for carbon deposits or blockage; clean with a tip cleaner/reamer tool. Coil hoses carefully and hang safely.
- **Daily**: Check all hose connections for leaks using soapy water; verify regulator gauges read zero when cylinder valves are closed and torch valves are open.
- **Weekly**: Inspect hoses for cuts, abrasions, and heat damage along their full length; replace if any degradation is found.
- **Monthly**: Test flashback arrestors: with low pressure and no flame, they should allow gas flow in the correct direction only; replace if they fail the flow test.
- **As needed**: Replace worn hose crimps or fittings; re-seal any leaking regulator connections with thread tape rated for oxygen service.
- **Cylinder management**: Monitor gas levels using regulator low-pressure gauge; arrange cylinder exchange before running out mid-session. Store empty cylinders separately from full ones, valve-capped.
- **Torch tip care**: Never use steel tools to clean tip orifices; use the correct-size soft tip cleaner to avoid enlarging the orifice, which changes the flame character.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What is a backfire and what causes it?**
A backfire is a sudden extinguishment of the flame accompanied by a sharp "pop," followed by possible reignition at the tip. Causes include: the tip overheating and the flame burning back into the tip, slag or workpiece contact with the tip, or insufficient gas pressure. To respond: close both valves immediately, allow the tip to cool, and inspect for blockage before relighting. A sustained backfire (flame burning inside the torch) requires immediate valve closure and is a precursor to a flashback.

**Q: What is the difference between brazing and welding?**
Welding involves melting and fusing the base metal itself. Brazing joins metals using a filler metal (typically bronze or silver alloy) whose melting point is above 450 °C but below the melting point of the base metal. The base metal does not melt; instead, the molten filler flows into the joint by capillary action. Brazing can join dissimilar metals (e.g., copper to steel) and produces less distortion than fusion welding, but the joint strength depends on the filler and joint clearance (0.025–0.1 mm optimal).

**Q: Why is my flame orange and smoky when I try to light it?**
An orange, sooty flame indicates insufficient oxygen is reaching the tip, producing incomplete combustion of the acetylene. This is the normal appearance of a pure acetylene flame before oxygen is added — it is correct. Simply open the oxygen valve progressively until the sooty orange transitions to a bright, white inner cone. If the flame remains smoky with oxygen fully open, the tip orifice may be partially blocked.

**Q: Can I use oxy-LPG instead of oxy-acetylene for welding?**
Oxy-LPG (propane or butane) flames reach approximately 2,800 °C, compared to 3,100–3,500 °C for oxy-acetylene. This is sufficient for cutting and brazing but is generally inadequate for fusion welding of steel because the lower flame temperature produces excessive heat-affected zones and makes controlling the pool more difficult. Oxy-LPG is preferred for cutting and heating applications due to its lower cost and greater safety margin compared to acetylene.

**Q: Why does my aluminium workpiece suddenly collapse through when I try to weld it?**
Aluminium retains its solid appearance almost up to its melting point (~660 °C) and then transitions very rapidly to liquid, with no visible red-hot phase as with steel. Without practice, the welder fails to notice the pool forming and applies excess heat. When welding aluminium, use flux to clear the oxide layer, use a larger tip than you would for equivalent-thickness steel, keep the torch moving constantly, and watch for the surface to lose its solid sheen — this is your cue to add filler and advance.
