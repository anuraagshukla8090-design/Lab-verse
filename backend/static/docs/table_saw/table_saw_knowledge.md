# Table Saw

## Overview

A table saw is a stationary woodworking machine in which a circular saw blade is mounted on an arbor below a flat, rigid table surface, with the blade projecting upward through a slot (kerf) in the table. The workpiece is fed horizontally across the table into the rotating blade, guided by either the rip fence (for cuts parallel to the grain) or the miter gauge (for cross cuts at a fixed angle). The table saw is arguably the most versatile and productive woodworking machine in a laboratory or workshop, capable of performing rip cuts, crosscuts, bevel cuts, dado cuts, and rabbet cuts with high accuracy and repeatability.

In a mechanical laboratory, table saws are used to dimension timber for jigs, fixtures, form-work, test specimen frames, and packing structures. The machine's adjustable blade height and bevel tilt (typically 0–45°) give it the flexibility to handle a wide range of tasks. Cabinet saws, contractor saws, and hybrid saws represent different classes defined by motor power, weight, and fence accuracy; most laboratory environments use contractor or hybrid saws in the 1.5–3 kW (2–4 HP) range.

The table saw is also one of the most dangerous machines in any workshop, with kickback—where the workpiece is suddenly propelled back toward the operator at high speed—being the leading cause of serious injuries. Understanding kickback causes and prevention, correct blade guard and riving knife usage, and proper body positioning are essential knowledge for every table saw operator.

## Working Principle

The table saw's electric motor drives a horizontal arbor through a belt-and-pulley system (or direct drive in some models), rotating the circular saw blade at a fixed speed of typically 3,000–4,500 RPM. The arbor assembly is mounted on a trunnion mechanism that allows two key adjustments: blade height (raising or lowering the arbor) and blade tilt (tilting the arbor left or right for bevel cuts, typically 0–45°).

When the workpiece is pushed into the blade, the carbide-tipped teeth cut through the material in an upward direction at the front of the blade. This upward tooth motion presses the workpiece firmly against the table—beneficial for control—but it also means that if the workpiece binds against the blade (due to the kerf closing), the blade can grab the workpiece and throw it backward toward the operator at velocities exceeding 150 km/h. The riving knife (or splitter), mounted directly behind the blade in the kerf, holds the kerf open to prevent this binding and is the primary anti-kickback device.

## Main Components

| Component | Function |
|---|---|
| Table Top | Flat, precision-ground cast iron or aluminium surface that provides a reference plane for the workpiece; contains the throat plate insert around the blade |
| Circular Saw Blade | Carbide-tipped steel disc that performs the cutting; available in different tooth counts for ripping (fewer teeth) or crosscutting (more teeth) |
| Blade Guard Assembly | Transparent polycarbonate or metal cover over the blade that prevents contact with the spinning blade during normal cuts |
| Riving Knife (Splitter) | Fixed or floating steel plate mounted directly behind the blade to hold the kerf open and prevent the workpiece from pinching the blade—the primary kickback prevention device |
| Anti-Kickback Pawls | Spring-loaded serrated steel fingers mounted behind the blade that allow the workpiece to travel forward but dig in to stop backward movement during kickback |
| Rip Fence | Parallel guide clamped to the table rails that maintains a constant distance between the fence face and the blade for rip cuts |
| Miter Gauge | T-shaped sliding guide that fits into table slots and can be set to any angle for accurate crosscuts and miter cuts |
| Blade Height Adjustment Handwheel | Raises or lowers the arbor trunnion to set how high the blade projects above the table surface |
| Bevel Adjustment Handwheel | Tilts the arbor and blade left (or right on some models) from 0° to 45° for bevel and compound cuts |
| Fence Rails and Locking Lever | Precision steel rails along the front and rear of the table on which the rip fence slides and locks; accurate parallelism with the blade is essential |

## Inputs and Outputs

### Inputs
- Electrical power (typically 240V single-phase, 1.5–3 kW)
- Timber or sheet workpiece of appropriate size (not exceeding the table's capacity)
- Blade height adjustment: blade should project approximately 6–10 mm above the workpiece surface
- Rip fence set to the desired rip width, or miter gauge set to the required angle
- Operator push force to feed the workpiece through the blade

### Outputs
- Ripped, crosscut, bevelled, or dado-cut workpiece at the specified dimension and angle
- Offcut piece (the waste portion released after the cut)
- Sawdust and wood chips (collected by dust extraction if connected)
- Kerf waste (material removed as sawdust equal to the blade kerf width, typically 3–3.5 mm)

## Operating Procedure

1. **Inspect the blade**: mount the correct blade for the task (rip blade for long-grain ripping, crosscut blade for end grain); ensure it is sharp, undamaged, and the arbor nut is torqued correctly.
2. **Install the riving knife**: confirm the riving knife is properly seated and within 8 mm of the back of the blade at table height; never operate without it for through-cuts.
3. **Install the blade guard**: lower the guard over the blade; verify it rises freely when the workpiece is fed under it.
4. **Check anti-kickback pawls** for free pivoting action and sharp teeth; pawls must swing downward to engage the workpiece if it begins to move backward.
5. **Set the blade height**: raise the blade so it projects 6–10 mm above the workpiece thickness; excessive blade height increases kickback risk and injury severity.
6. **Set the rip fence or miter gauge**: for rip cuts, set the fence to the required width and lock it; for crosscuts, set the miter gauge to the required angle.
7. **Don PPE**: safety glasses, hearing protection, and a dust mask; do not wear gloves (they reduce grip feel and can catch the blade).
8. **Start the saw motor** and allow the blade to reach full speed before introducing the workpiece.
9. **Stand to the side of the blade line**, never directly behind the blade where kickback projectiles travel.
10. **Feed the workpiece steadily** into the blade using push sticks or featherboards for narrow rip cuts (whenever the rip width is less than 150 mm, use a push stick).
11. **Maintain constant, even feed rate**; do not pause mid-cut—a stationary workpiece against a spinning blade causes burning and increases binding risk.
12. **Use a push stick to complete the cut** past the blade; never reach over or behind the blade with bare hands to retrieve the offcut.
13. **Wait for the blade to stop spinning** before reaching across the table to retrieve workpieces or making any adjustments.
14. **Turn off the saw and wait for full blade stop** before changing blade height, bevel angle, or performing any maintenance.
15. **Remove offcuts and sawdust** from the table and floor promptly; clean the fence rails and miter gauge slots with a brush or vacuum.

## Safety Rules

1. **Never remove or bypass the blade guard, riving knife, or anti-kickback pawls** for standard through-cuts; these are life-safety devices, not optional accessories.
2. **Always stand to the side of the blade line**, not directly behind it; a kicked-back workpiece can cause fatal injury.
3. **Use a push stick for any rip cut where the rip width is less than 150 mm**; never guide the workpiece past the blade with bare hands.
4. **Never reach over a running blade** for any reason; turn off the motor and wait for the blade to fully stop before accessing the table.
5. **Never perform freehand cuts** (without the rip fence or miter gauge); freehand feeding creates uncontrolled blade contact and causes kickback.
6. **Ensure the rip fence is parallel to the blade** within 0.1 mm; a toe-in fence causes the kerf to close behind the blade and triggers kickback.
7. **Never use the rip fence and miter gauge simultaneously** for the same cut; the offcut can become trapped between the fence and blade, causing ejection.
8. **Do not cut warped, twisted, or badly bowed lumber** without straightening it first on a jointer; the uneven surface causes unpredictable blade contact.
9. **Always disconnect the power** before changing blades, adjusting the riving knife, or performing any maintenance; never rely on the power switch alone.
10. **Keep the blade sharp**: a dull blade requires more feed force, generates excess heat, increases the tendency to bind, and multiplies kickback risk.
11. **Never cut metal or masonry** with a wood blade; use the appropriate machine and blade for the material.
12. **Connect dust extraction** before operating: fine wood dust is a respiratory hazard and a fire/explosion risk when concentrated in air.

## Common Applications

- **Ripping lumber to width** for jig bases, workbench rails, and fixture frames in the mechanical lab
- **Crosscutting timber to precise length** for test specimen supports, material handling frames, and form-work
- **Cutting plywood and MDF sheets** into panels for shelving, partitioning, and display boards
- **Bevelling timber edges** at 45° for mitre-joint frames, specimen containment boxes, and corner joints
- **Producing dado and rabbet joints** using dado blade sets for lap joints and housing joints in jig construction
- **Resawing thick timber** into thinner boards for lightweight fixture components
- **Cutting timber wedges and tapers** using a tapering jig on the rip fence for custom support structures
- **Sizing form-work timber** for concrete pour moulds used in civil engineering specimen preparation

## Maintenance

- **Before each use**: inspect the blade for missing or chipped carbide teeth; a blade with damaged teeth must be removed and resharpened or replaced.
- **Daily**: clean sawdust from the table surface, fence rails, miter gauge slots, and blade guard using a brush and vacuum; accumulated dust can cause fence misalignment.
- **Weekly**: wax the cast-iron table surface with paste wax (not silicone-based) to reduce friction and prevent rust; buff to a thin, smooth film.
- **Weekly**: check and adjust the rip fence for parallelism to the blade using a reliable square or dial indicator; correct any toe-in or toe-out.
- **Monthly**: inspect the drive belt for cracking, glazing, or fraying; a slipping belt causes speed variation and blade stalling.
- **Monthly**: lubricate the arbor elevation and tilt trunnion threads with dry lubricant or paste wax to keep the handwheels operating smoothly.
- **Quarterly**: have saw blades professionally cleaned and re-sharpened; carbide teeth cleaned of pitch and resin improve cutting quality significantly.
- **Annually**: check arbor bearings for play or roughness; worn bearings cause blade wobble, poor cut quality, and unsafe operating conditions.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q:** What causes kickback and how can it be prevented?
**A:** Kickback occurs when the blade is pinched by the workpiece—either because the kerf closes (wood tension), the cut piece swings back into the blade, or the rip fence is misaligned. Prevention relies on four mechanisms working together: the riving knife keeps the kerf open; the anti-kickback pawls grip the workpiece if it moves rearward; correct blade height minimises blade exposure; and the operator standing to the side ensures safety if kickback occurs despite these measures.

**Q:** What blade should I use for ripping versus crosscutting?
**A:** A rip blade has 24–30 flat-top-ground (FTG) teeth with deep gullets designed to clear large chip volumes quickly along the grain. A crosscut blade has 60–80 alternate-top-bevel (ATB) teeth designed for clean, splinter-free cuts across the grain. A combination (combo) blade with 40–50 teeth can perform both tasks adequately for general lab use.

**Q:** How high should the blade be set above the workpiece?
**A:** The blade should project 6–10 mm above the top surface of the workpiece (approximately one tooth height). Higher projection reduces the blade's approach angle, minimises splintering on the top face, reduces heat generation, and—importantly—reduces the severity of any accidental contact with the blade.

**Q:** Can I use the rip fence and miter gauge at the same time?
**A:** No. Never use the rip fence as a stop for crosscuts made with the miter gauge. As the cut completes, the offcut becomes trapped between the fence and blade, is grabbed by the blade, and is violently ejected. If you need a stop for crosscuts, clamp a wooden stop block to the fence at a safe distance upstream of the blade.

**Q:** Why does my workpiece burn along the cut?
**A:** Burning is caused by a dull blade (insufficient cutting speed per tooth), feeding too slowly (dwelling against the blade), or a blade with too many teeth for ripping. Use a sharp rip blade, maintain a steady and appropriately fast feed rate, and clean resin buildup from the blade teeth with a blade-cleaning solvent.
